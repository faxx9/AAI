#  **Question 1: How Snow Moved from Correlation to Causation**

Snow didn’t stop at “many deaths happened near the Broad Street pump.” That alone is just correlation. To argue causation, he took several **deliberate, scientific steps**:

### 🔍 **1. He collected *individual-level* data, not just neighborhood-level data**
He knocked on doors, interviewed families, and mapped **exact addresses** of victims.  
This allowed him to see patterns that weren’t visible in aggregate data.

###  **2. He investigated *exceptions* — people near the pump who *didn’t* get sick**
This is crucial for causation.  
He found:
- A brewery near the pump where workers drank beer instead of water → **almost no cholera cases**.
- A workhouse near the pump with its own well → **very few cases**.

These exceptions strengthened the argument that **water source**, not location, was the key variable.

###  **3. He traced cases *far from* the pump back to Broad Street**
Some victims lived miles away but **preferred the taste** of Broad Street water and had it delivered.  
This reversed the correlation:  
People weren’t dying because they lived near the pump —  
**they died because they drank from it.**

###  **4. He compared water companies with different water sources**
In a separate study, he compared:
- A company drawing water downstream from sewage → high cholera rates  
- A company drawing water upstream → low cholera rates  

Same neighborhoods, same people, different water → **strong causal evidence**.

###  **5. He recommended removing the pump handle**
When cholera cases dropped after the pump was disabled, it provided **real-world experimental confirmation**.

**Together, these steps transformed a correlation into a compelling causal argument.**

# 📉 **Question 2: What If Snow Only Used Hospital Data?**

If Snow had relied solely on hospital records, his dataset would have been:

### ❌ **Biased**
Hospitals only captured:
- People sick enough to seek treatment  
- People who could afford care  
- People who lived close enough to reach a hospital  

He would miss:
- Entire households that died at home  
- Poorer families  
- Rapid deaths that never reached medical care  
- Spatial patterns across streets and buildings  

###  **Less granular**
Hospital data would show:
- “X patients admitted with cholera”  
But not:
- Which pump they used  
- Their exact home location  
- Household-level clusters  
- Exceptions (brewery, workhouse, etc.)

###  **More confounded**
Hospitals are located in specific places.  
He might have concluded:
- “People near hospitals have more cholera”  
Which is obviously false — it’s just where the sick people go.

### **Bottom line**
Without door-to-door data, Snow would likely have:
- Missed the Broad Street pump as the epicenter  
- Failed to identify water as the causal mechanism  
- Possibly reinforced the miasma theory  

His breakthrough depended on **complete, unbiased, street-level data**.

#  **Question 3: A Modern “Miasma” — A Widely Accepted but Flawed Assumption**

A “miasma” today is any belief that:
- Sounds intuitive  
- Is widely accepted  
- Explains surface-level patterns  
- But collapses under rigorous data analysis  

Here are strong modern examples:

---

## 📱 **Example: “More screen time causes worse mental health in teens.”**

This is widely believed, but data shows:
- Correlation is extremely small  
- Reverse causation is plausible (struggling teens use phones more)  
- Confounding variables (sleep, family stress, socioeconomic status) explain more variance  

It *feels* true — just like miasma — but the data is messy and far from conclusive.

---

## 🏢 **Example: “Open office plans increase collaboration.”**

Companies spent billions redesigning offices based on this assumption.  
But rigorous studies show:
- Face‑to‑face interactions *decrease*  
- Productivity drops  
- Stress increases  
- Employees use headphones and digital messaging to avoid noise  

It looked right on the surface (“open space = open communication”), but data disproved it.

---

## 💼 **Example: “More hours worked = more productivity.”**

Still common in business culture, but data shows:
- Productivity per hour drops sharply after ~50 hours/week  
- Error rates increase  
- Burnout rises  
- Long-term output decreases  

It’s a modern miasma: intuitive, widespread, and wrong.
